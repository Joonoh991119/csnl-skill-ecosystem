## Visual Working Memory Capacity Limits / 시각적 작업 기억 용량 한계

### One-Sentence Summary

Visual working memory can hold only 3-4 objects simultaneously because the brain stores information in discrete "slots" (Luck & Vogel, 1997) or as a shared resource pool (Zhang & Luck, 2008), and this capacity limit is robust across display complexity and object features [Cowan, 2001].

### Core Explanation

Visual working memory (VWM) is your ability to temporarily hold and manipulate visual information without looking at it—like remembering the position of a coffee cup after you glance away from a table. One of the most striking discoveries in cognitive neuroscience is that VWM has a severe capacity bottleneck: it can store approximately 3-4 items before performance breaks down. This limitation was first quantitatively demonstrated by Luck & Vogel (1997) through elegant change detection experiments, where participants saw arrays of colored squares and had to detect if any square changed color after a brief delay. Their results revealed a remarkable constant: the number of items an observer could remember (called K, the capacity estimate) plateaued at roughly 3.5 items, regardless of whether the array contained 4, 6, or 8 squares.

This capacity limit sparked one of the most fundamental debates in working memory research: what causes it? Two competing theories emerged. The slot model (Luck & Vogel, 1997) proposes that VWM operates like a fixed set of storage bins—you have approximately 3-4 discrete "slots," and each slot holds one complete object with all its features. Once your slots are full, you cannot remember any additional items. In contrast, the resource model (Zhang & Luck, 2008) suggests that VWM uses a flexible pool of representational resources. Rather than dividing capacity into separate slots, VWM distributes a fixed amount of "attentional resource" across all items in the display. More items means less precision per item; fewer items means better precision. Both models predict the 3-4 item limit, but through fundamentally different mechanisms. Cowan's (2001) review synthesized evidence showing that this capacity constraint persists across different stimulus types, task demands, and attentional manipulations—suggesting the limit reflects a core architectural feature of the visual system itself.

The distinction matters for understanding how your brain prioritizes what to remember. In a slot-based system, you either remember an item completely or not at all—it's all-or-nothing storage. In a resource-based system, all items are stored, but with variable precision; an item you're not paying attention to is just a blurry representation. Recent evidence increasingly suggests both mechanisms may operate in parallel, with the slot mechanism dominating when items are in focus and resource flexibility becoming more important when attention is divided [Zhang & Luck, 2008].

### Key Terms

- **Visual working memory (VWM)**: The cognitive system that temporarily holds and manipulates visual information for 1-3 seconds without sensory input. [Source: Luck & Vogel, 1997; Cowan, 2001]

- **Capacity limit**: The maximum number of distinct items VWM can simultaneously represent, typically 3-4 items as measured by K (capacity estimate). [Source: Luck & Vogel, 1997]

- **Slot model**: A theoretical framework proposing that VWM has a fixed number of discrete storage slots (approximately 3-4), each holding one object with all its features. Once slots are filled, additional items cannot be remembered. [Source: Luck & Vogel, 1997]

- **Resource model**: A theoretical framework proposing that VWM uses a flexible pool of representational resources distributed across all items in working memory. Capacity limits arise from dividing finite resources among items. [Source: Zhang & Luck, 2008]

- **Change detection**: An experimental paradigm where observers view a visual array, experience a brief blank period, then see a test array and judge whether any item changed. Errors in change detection reveal VWM capacity. [Source: Luck & Vogel, 1997]

- **K estimate (capacity estimate)**: A quantitative measure of VWM capacity calculated from change detection performance; reflects the number of items an observer can remember (typically 3-4). [Source: Luck & Vogel, 1997]

- **Set size**: The number of items in a visual display; in capacity experiments, set sizes typically range from 2 to 8 items. [Source: Luck & Vogel, 1997]

- **Precision**: The fidelity of a visual representation; in the resource model, precision decreases as more items share the same pool of resources. [Source: Zhang & Luck, 2008]

### Visual Analogy

Think of VWM capacity like a juggler. A skilled juggler can smoothly juggle 3-4 balls at once, keeping them all in the air with clear, sharp trajectories. If you try to add a 5th or 6th ball, the juggler either drops balls (slot model: information is lost) or becomes less precise—the balls get thrown at slightly wrong angles and nearly collide (resource model: all information is encoded but with lower precision). The critical insight is that the limitation isn't about how hard the juggler tries or how clever they are; it's baked into the physical mechanics of juggling itself. Similarly, the VWM capacity limit isn't a failure of attention or motivation—it reflects the architecture of the visual system.

### Connection to Other Concepts

- **Related to: Attention**: VWM capacity limits are closely tied to the distribution of visual attention. Where you focus attention determines which items get high-precision representations in VWM [Cowan, 2001]. Attention acts as a gate controlling which items enter the high-capacity-cost slots.

- **Builds on: Iconic memory**: VWM is a later processing stage than iconic memory (the initial visual buffer that decays in ~500ms). The capacity limit of VWM is more restrictive than iconic memory's high capacity, suggesting information gets "compressed" as it moves from iconic memory to working memory [Luck & Vogel, 1997].

- **Leads to: Cognitive load effects**: The capacity limits of VWM explain why performance degrades when you must hold multiple items (e.g., memorizing a phone number while reading). Once capacity is exceeded, new items displace old ones or reduce precision [Zhang & Luck, 2008].

- **Contrasts with: Long-term memory**: Unlike VWM's strict 3-4 item limit, long-term memory has essentially unlimited capacity. This distinction highlights how working memory is fundamentally a gating mechanism that shields long-term memory from irrelevant information.

### Sources

1. **Luck, S. J., & Vogel, E. K. (1997).** "The capacity of visual working memory for features and conjunctions." *Nature*, 390(6657), 279-281. **Primary source for slot model and K capacity estimates.** This landmark study introduced the change detection paradigm and first quantified the 3-4 item limit.

2. **Cowan, N. (2001).** "The magical number 4 in short-term memory: A reconsideration of mental storage capacity." *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 24(1), 87-185. **Comprehensive review synthesizing evidence for capacity limits across modalities.** Cowan's analysis showed the limit is robust and appears to reflect a core architectural constraint.

3. **Zhang, W., & Luck, S. J. (2008).** "Discrete fixed-resolution representations in visual working memory." *Nature*, 453(7192), 233-235. **Key evidence for resource model and precision-based storage.** This paper demonstrated that VWM can achieve higher accuracy with fewer items (precision increase), supporting resource allocation mechanisms.
